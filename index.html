Properties of Matter

Inquiry question: How do the properties of substances help us to classify and separate them?
  • 1. Explore homogeneous mixtures and heterogeneous mixtures through practical investigations
    Homogenous mixtures: Two or more components in uniform distribution
    • Liquid + liquid (miscible)
      1. Distillation (boiling point) → (Only two liquids → One product)
      1. Fractional distillation (boiling point) → (More liquids → Multiple products)
        • How it works:
          1. Vaporization: The mixture is heated in a flask, causing the more volatile (lower boiling point) components to evaporate first.
          1. Condensation & Re-Evaporation: The vapor rises through the fractionating column, which is filled with plates, glass beads, or other materials that increase surface area. As the vapor moves up, it cools slightly, causing some of the higher-boiling components to condense and fall back into the flask.
          1. Separation: This repeated condensation and evaporation process allows the lower-boiling component to become more concentrated in the vapor phase as it moves higher in the column.
          1. Collection: The purified vapor reaches the top of the column, where it is directed to a condenser and collected as a liquid.

    • Liquid + solid (soluble)
      1. Evaporation (boiling point) → Solvent not required
      1. Distillation (boiling point) → Solvent required
    Heterogenous mixtures: Two or more components in non-uniform distribution
    • Solid + solid
      1. Sieving (particle size)
      1. Magnetism (magnetism) → (For iron, cobalt, nickel)
    • Liquid + solid (insoluble)
      1. Filtration (particle size)
      1. Centrifugation (density) → Only provides qualitative separation
      1. Sedimentation (density)→ Only provides qualitative separation
    • Liquid + liquid (immiscible)
      1. Decantation (density)
    Gravimetric Analysis:
    • A means of quantitative analysis to determine mixture composition
    • Extract pure samples of each component from the mixture
    Practical: Gravimetric Analysis on Shell Grit and Salt Mixture

    Aim: Separate salt/shell grit mixture and weigh components

    Setup: Insoluble shell grit and soluble salt mixture (solid+solid)

    Procedure:

    • Water poured in to mixture until all salt dissolved
    • Filtration performed to remove shell grit from solution (stored until dry)
    • Remaining solution evaporated in basin until all water evaporated
    • Salt left over was weighed, compared to original
    • Shell grit weighed and compared to original

    Discussion:

    • Why were results unexpected?
      • Shell grit was heavier since it was still wet
      • Salt was lighter since some was lost during filtration and sputtering when evaporating
    Practical: Separating an Acetone + Salt Water mixture

    Aim: Separate a liquid + liquid (immiscible) solution using a separating funnel (decantation)

    Setup: Acetone and salt water in a separating funnel (Immiscible liquids form distinct layers separated by an interface)

    Procedure:

    • Mixture poured into funnel from top, then shaken and left to settle
      • Excess gas released by loosening cap
    • Dial was turned slowly to allow the salt water to pour out into a beaker (cap must be off)
    • Line was observed until it was barely above the dial
      • Ensure layer that is more valuable is completely collected (even if diluted)
    • Remainder (acetone) was poured into another beaker from top of funnel
    Practical: Separating an Chai + Water mixture

    Aim: Separate a homogenous solid + liquid solution using distillation

    Setup: Chai and water in a pear-shaped flash

    Procedure:

    • Mixture kept in a pear-shaped flask with boiling chips added, then secured on retort stand
    • Condenser connected to tap and cool water run through
    • Bunsen burner lit underneath flask
    • Liquid boiled eventually, all water was collected in conical flask and chai solid remained

Atomic structure and atomic mass

Inquiry question: Why are atoms of elements different from one another?
  • 1. Investigate the basic structure of stable and unstable isotopes by examining:
    • Periodic table position (Band of Stability):
    • Distribution of subatomic particles
      Isotopic Stability:
      • Protons cannot exist so close to each other in the nucleus since they repel each other
        • Strong nuclear force holds nucleons together over very small distances (strongest force in the universe)
        • Weak electrostatic force causes protons (+) to repel each other, so the nucleus becomes unstable if the ratio of protons is to too high
      • Determined by size of nucleus and composition (n:p ratio)
        • For elements with smaller nuclei, n:p ratio should be around 1:1
        • For larger elements like uranium, the ratio should be around 1.5:1
          • Heavier elements have larger nuclei, so it becomes more difficult to arrange protons such that they minimise repulsive interactions, so larger elements have more neutrons relative to protons compared to smaller elements
    • Representations (symbol, atomic and mass numbers)
      Isotopes: Different forms of the same element with different numbers of neutrons.

      Isotopic Notation

      • Gives atomic composition for one isotope of an element

      Periodic Table

      • Gives atomic composition as a function of natural abundance of all isotopes for that element
      • Contains decimals to indicate abundance

  • 5. Explore the similarities and differences between the nature of intermolecular and intramolecular bonds and the strength of the forces associated with each, in order to explain the:
    1. Nature of intermolecular forces and intramolecular bonds
      • Intermolecular forces - Attraction between molecules. Not chemical or physical. Related to physical change in state.
      • Intramolecular forces - Forces involved in chemical bonds within molecules. Relatively strong.
        • Bonds present with all chemical structures.
        • In this case, referring to materials that do not form molecules, but rather large structures (networks) with a repeating pattern, eg. crystal lattices
        • Interactions in network structures consist of electrostatic attractions between full charges, as opposed to the partial charges observed between molecules.
        • This accounts for the substantially higher melting and boiling points observed.
